How to Compare New Policies After Outliving Term Life

Home / Blog / Blog Details

Term life insurance is a straightforward and affordable way to protect your loved ones financially. But what happens when your term expires, and you’re still alive and kicking? Many policyholders face this dilemma—especially as life expectancy increases and financial needs evolve. Comparing new policies after outliving term life requires careful consideration of your current situation, future goals, and the ever-changing insurance landscape.

Understanding Your Post-Term Life Insurance Needs

Before diving into new policies, assess why you still need coverage. Term life insurance is often purchased to cover specific liabilities—like a mortgage, college tuition, or income replacement. If those obligations no longer exist, you might not need as much (or any) coverage. However, if you still have dependents, debts, or estate planning concerns, a new policy could be essential.

Key Questions to Ask Yourself:

  • Has your financial situation changed? (e.g., higher net worth, new debts)
  • Do you still have dependents? (e.g., children, a non-working spouse)
  • Are you planning for legacy or estate taxes?
  • How’s your health compared to when you first bought term life?

Types of Policies to Consider After Term Life Expires

1. Renewing or Converting Your Term Policy

Some term policies offer renewal or conversion options. Renewing lets you extend coverage (often at a higher premium), while converting switches your term policy to permanent life insurance (e.g., whole or universal life) without a medical exam.

Pros:
- No new underwriting if converting.
- Guaranteed coverage if renewing.

Cons:
- Renewal premiums can skyrocket.
- Permanent life insurance is more expensive than term.

2. Purchasing a New Term Life Policy

If you’re still in good health, buying a new term policy might be the most cost-effective option. However, premiums will be higher now that you’re older.

Pros:
- Lower cost than permanent insurance.
- Flexibility to match new term lengths (e.g., 10, 20 years).

Cons:
- Requires medical underwriting (health issues could disqualify you).
- Premiums increase with age.

3. Whole Life or Universal Life Insurance

Permanent policies provide lifelong coverage and cash value growth. They’re ideal if you want to leave a legacy or cover final expenses.

Pros:
- Lifelong protection.
- Cash value can be borrowed against.

Cons:
- Expensive compared to term.
- Complex fee structures.

4. Final Expense or Guaranteed Issue Life Insurance

If health is a concern, these policies offer smaller death benefits (usually $5K–$25K) with no medical exam.

Pros:
- Easy approval.
- Covers funeral costs and small debts.

Cons:
- Low coverage amounts.
- Higher premiums per dollar of coverage.

Comparing Costs: Term vs. Permanent vs. No Exam Policies

Use this simplified comparison table to weigh your options:

| Policy Type | Avg. Monthly Cost (Age 60) | Coverage Duration | Medical Underwriting? |
|----------------------|---------------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|
| New 20-Year Term | $150–$300 | 20 years | Yes |
| Whole Life | $400–$800 | Lifetime | Sometimes |
| Guaranteed Issue | $50–$150 | Lifetime | No |

Note: Costs vary by insurer, health, and coverage amount.

How Health and Age Impact Your Options

If You’re Healthy:

  • Shop aggressively for new term or permanent policies.
  • Lock in lower rates before age-related premium hikes.

If You Have Health Issues:

  • Explore guaranteed issue or simplified underwriting policies.
  • Consider converting an existing term policy (if available).

If You’re Over 70:

  • Focus on final expense or small whole life policies.
  • Avoid long-term commitments unless necessary.

The Role of Inflation and Economic Trends

Today’s economic climate—marked by inflation, rising interest rates, and market volatility—affects life insurance:
- Higher interest rates = Better returns on cash value policies.
- Inflation = May require larger death benefits to maintain purchasing power.
- Insurer stability = Research companies with strong financial ratings (e.g., A.M. Best).

Pitfalls to Avoid When Switching Policies

  1. Lapsing Your Old Policy Too Soon
    • Secure new coverage before canceling the old one.
  2. Overestimating Cash Value Growth
    • Permanent policies grow slowly; don’t rely on them for short-term needs.
  3. Ignoring Riders
    • Look for critical illness or long-term care riders if health is a concern.

Final Thoughts on Making the Right Choice

There’s no one-size-fits-all answer. Your best move depends on health, budget, and goals. Work with a licensed agent, compare quotes, and prioritize flexibility. Whether you opt for a new term policy, convert to permanent coverage, or scale back to final expense insurance, the key is aligning your choice with your evolving life stage.

Remember: Life insurance isn’t just about death—it’s about living with peace of mind.
