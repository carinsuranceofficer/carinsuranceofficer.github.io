How Much Is Car Insurance for High-Risk Drivers?

Home / Blog / Blog Details

Car insurance is a necessity for all drivers, but for those labeled as "high-risk," the costs can be staggering. Whether due to a history of accidents, DUIs, speeding tickets, or poor credit, high-risk drivers face significantly higher premiums. In today’s world, where economic pressures and road safety concerns are at an all-time high, understanding the factors that influence these rates is crucial.

What Makes a Driver High-Risk?

Insurance companies assess risk based on several factors. The more risk you pose, the higher your premiums will be. Here are the most common reasons drivers are classified as high-risk:

1. Traffic Violations and Accidents

Multiple speeding tickets, at-fault accidents, or reckless driving convictions signal to insurers that you’re more likely to file a claim. Even a single DUI can skyrocket your rates for years.

2. Lack of Driving Experience

Teen drivers and those with limited experience are statistically more prone to accidents. Insurance companies charge higher premiums to offset this risk.

3. Poor Credit Score

In many states, insurers use credit-based insurance scores to determine rates. A low score suggests financial instability, which insurers correlate with higher claim likelihood.

4. Lapses in Coverage

If you’ve gone without insurance for an extended period, insurers may see you as a higher risk, leading to increased premiums when you reapply.

5. Driving a High-Performance or Expensive Vehicle

Sports cars and luxury vehicles cost more to insure due to their repair costs and higher theft rates.

How Much More Do High-Risk Drivers Pay?

The exact cost varies widely by state, driving record, and insurer, but high-risk drivers can expect to pay at least double the national average.

Average Annual Premiums for High-Risk Drivers

  • Clean Record (Baseline): ~$1,500
  • With One At-Fault Accident: ~$2,200
  • With a DUI: ~$3,000+
  • Multiple Violations: $4,000+

State-by-State Variations

States like Michigan and Florida have some of the highest premiums due to no-fault insurance laws and high uninsured driver rates. Meanwhile, states like Maine and Ohio tend to be more affordable.

Ways to Lower High-Risk Insurance Costs

While high-risk drivers face steep premiums, there are strategies to reduce costs:

1. Shop Around and Compare Quotes

Not all insurers penalize high-risk drivers equally. Some specialize in non-standard auto insurance and offer better rates.

2. Take a Defensive Driving Course

Completing an approved course can demonstrate responsibility and may qualify you for discounts.

3. Opt for a Higher Deductible

Choosing a higher out-of-pocket deductible can lower monthly premiums—just ensure you can afford it if an accident occurs.

4. Improve Your Credit Score

Paying bills on time and reducing debt can gradually improve your credit, leading to better insurance rates.

5. Consider Usage-Based Insurance

Programs like Progressive’s Snapshot or State Farm’s Drive Safe & Save track driving habits and reward safe behavior with discounts.

The Future of High-Risk Insurance

With advancements in telematics and AI, insurers are increasingly using real-time data to assess risk. Safe drivers—even those with past violations—may soon see more personalized and fairer pricing. Additionally, legislative changes in some states aim to reduce penalties for minor infractions, potentially easing the burden on high-risk drivers.

For now, the best approach is to drive safely, maintain continuous coverage, and explore every available option to minimize costs. The road to lower premiums may be long, but it’s not impossible.
